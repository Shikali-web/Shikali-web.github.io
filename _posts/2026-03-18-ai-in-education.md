Artificial intelligence is quietly reshaping classrooms around the world — from AI tutors that adapt to each student's pace, to tools that help teachers grade faster and identify struggling learners before they fall behind.

## Personalised learning at scale

One of the most promising applications is adaptive learning. AI systems like Khan Academy's Khanmigo can identify exactly where a student is stuck and adjust the difficulty of exercises in real time — something no single teacher can do for 40 students at once.

## The risks we cannot ignore

Not all the news is good. Over-reliance on AI tools risks eroding critical thinking skills. Students who outsource essay writing to AI miss the struggle that builds genuine understanding. There is also a deepening equity gap: schools with better internet access and devices benefit far more than those without.

## What the classroom of 2030 might look like

The teachers most likely to thrive are those who treat AI as an assistant, not a replacement. Lesson planning, administrative paperwork, and routine feedback can be delegated to AI — freeing teachers to do what they do best: inspire, mentor, and build human connection with students.

AI will not replace great teachers. But teachers who use AI effectively will replace those who do not.
